Mobile Application Design

Project Overview

Spotify is an online streaming media service that provides users access to a databases of content (i.e. artists, albums, tracks, playlists, etc). We specifically target veteran Spotify Users with 5+ years of daily use. We were able to identify usability trends, compile and analyze to create succinct implementable modifications.

This was a collaborative remote project containing 3 team members, myself included. We developed Heurstic Evaluations as well and completed detailed Competitive Feature Analysis with competitors (Pandora, SoundCloud, and YouTube Music).

While each team member was charged with specific tasks and responsibilities, we worked cohesively to present finalized offerings in 1 week's time with a $0 budget. I personally was able to conduct user interviews (based from a discussion guide created by another team member), plot, then synthesize this data through affinity mapping, and compile a distinct persona of the applicationâ€™s users.

Users and Audience

We specifically targeted veteran Spotify Users with 5+ years of daily use and were able to identify usability trends, and compile/analyze said trends to create succinct implementable modifications.

This project was designed to be presented to Stakeholders in an effort to improve consumer experience while maintaining established User Interface

Internal Audit Reveals Difficulties with Overwhelming Options.

We conducted a heuristic evaluation and competitive feature analysis to assess the current-state usability of the application. We explored the features competitors offered in their applications to better understand the market. We used these methods to gather a baseline understanding of the Spotify application and it's place in the market before speaking with actual users.

Upon examination, we discerned that the application, as it stood, was adequate for the average user and didn't have many usability issues. However, we noticed that there could be some improvements with ease of use options, specifically issues with the Match between System and Real World, Recognition vs Recall, and Help and Documentation. There were issues with word choice which confused users as well as application organization that did not lead to a flexible, easy to use experience.

We were able to identify several problem areas from our perspective then turned to users to gather data to see if these problems were shared issues with users or if other problems existed.

Veteran User Interviews Identify Usage Trends and Problem Areas.

As a team, with an understanding that the app was overall very well executed, we decided to reach out to veteran Spotify users to gather insightful data about user interactions with the app. In an effort to potentially uncover more untapped problem areas, we kept our research broad and let users describe in detail their experiences with the app in general.

We created a discussion guide with interview questions (including deep dive follow ups) to help navigate the conversations; maintaining a mindful perspective to ensure unbiased questioning and data.

Ultimately we interviewed 8 users, 4 males and 4 females ranging in age from 15 to 45. We were able to discuss with them in depth about their interactions with the application including their likes and dislikes

Synthesized Data and Compiled Persona of Spotify Users and Real-World Needs.

Once completing the interview process, we used affinity mapping to synthesize our collected data. All data points were identified and then grouped with similar points. Once everything had been grouped, clear and identifiable designations were created, thus establishing major insights collected from the interviews.

Ultimately we were able to use these collected insights to create a persona representative of our targeted user group; allowing us to more clearly see actual user struggles with an application previously thought more sound.

Our persona, Samantha, a 30yo Brand Marketer from the South, likes music, a lot. She listens on the go, through her trendy iPhone, at least 5 times a week, if not more. Because she travels for work, she likes to access her content offline also. She loves a good deal but doesn't mind paying for a service that she truly loves.

Utilizing Samantha we were able to develop a problem space to work in and swiftly begin crafting a tailored solution through beta-prototyping.

Conducted Usability Testing Uncovering Deficiencies and Documenting User Thought Processes.

We created a scenario and two tasks to see how users are currently navigating the app in an attempt to complete the task.

The first task asked users to locate a specific playlist from a specific subgenre of music featuring a specific artist. The goal of this was to see if users would attempt to to interact with the genre page of the application or discard that pathway entirely. Our testing indicated that users immediately went to the search bar, and 0 out of 6 users attempted to find the playlist using the genre/categories section.

The second task was designed to see how users would find a playlist given only certain information. They were given the playlist cover art, author, and genre tags, but were not given the actual name of the playlist. 5 out of 6 users immediately went to the profile page of the playlist creator. Hereâ€™s where things got interesting. In its current version, Spotify allows you to browse a profileâ€™s playlists, but it limits the number presented and also lacks a search feature. Users spent on average just shy of 4 minutes (3:54) unsuccessfully browsing the provided playlists before realizing they were unable to complete the task in that manner.

Proposed Unique Solutions using Low- then Mid-Fidelity Wireframes Specifically Tailored to Collected Data.

Each team member instinctively honed in on a particular problem identified through user testing. We ideated - using traditional UX strategies like Crazy 8s and hand sketching basic wireframes.

We then converted sketches into mid-fidelity digital wireframes to gain feedback from each other before finalizing our individual ideas in high-fidelity wireframes.

Although we each had individual ideas, we presented overall changes collectively. We agreed that stylistic changes should occur to minimize the design for the genres page. Our research indicated that users felt overwhelmed with the options so we minimized options and added a "see more" option for those interested in exploring through genres. We also made design changes to the profile page and added search capabilities.

Confabulated Solutions with Stakeholders to Gain Feedback and Further Iterate Solutions as necessary.

We assembled all of our research and findings to help our audience understand the problem at hand, then we shared each of our proposed solutions. The audience critiqued each design and gave each of us valuable feedback that we could all use in the future. Ultimately, they selected one idea as the one that best fit the needs of the user.

While my idea wasn't selected, I fully understood how my teammate's idea enhanced the user's experience AND had the added possibility of increasing the business' profits by possibly encouraging online shopping too.
