Responsive Design and Prototyping

Project Overview

Delta is a worldwide travel staple. Their name is synonymous with sophistication and class. They provide top-tier service and are known for their quality.

I was tasked with finding ways to improve the website - whether solving a usability problem or creating new features, the goal is to better the user experience while at the same time increase profitability.

I explored the website to uncover ways to improve it, but because I found no major usability violations, or lacking features, I turned to user study to discover if any current features were underrepresented or unutilized.

Users and Audience

While many people use the Delta.com website, I specifically targeted users that frequently flew the airline and also had experience using the site.

This project was designed to be presented to Stakeholders in an effort to increase revenue WHILE creating user friendly modifications

I spent 10 days researching the Website and it’s users to Determine deficiencies and areas where revenue could increase.

I completed user research, synthesized the research into easy-to-understand findings, and then used that info to generate new functionality that serves my persona/user group.

Throughout the process, there were several iterative moments - like with the problem statement and by doing multiple rounds of user research.

I completed this project independently, and remotely with the exception of some user testing and prototype testing which was conducted in-person at airports while waiting for flights.This project was for my UXD bootcamp course. It took place over a week with no budget.

An internal audit revealed virtually no usability issues and a website existing on equal ground with competitors.

I completed both a Heuristic Evaluation and Competitive Feature Analysis (American Airlines and Southwest Airlines) to gather an understanding of the Delta.com website and its place in the market before going to speak with actual users.

I promptly ascertained that the Website is actually very sound and that it doesn’t have many usability issues.

I also explored what features and offerings competitors are offering on their website and again promptly found that Delta.com is heavily competitive.

These discoveries provided a bit of turbulence for me. Up to this point I have worked with websites/applications that had distinct, identifiable problems right from the get-go. I had to adjust my tactics and really dive deep utilizing UX research to discover if/where improvements could be made.

User Interviews revealed Brand Loyalty but also Spotlighted unused site features.

I wanted to learn more about which features were most important to the user, understand the user’s motivations for using the website, and how users thought and experienced what they perceived as difficulties within the site.

I first determined criteria that defined my targeted users. Then, I wrote interview questions creating a discussion guide. I prepared possible follow-up questions and tried to anticipate possible answers without biasing the questions.

Next, we sourced people meeting the criteria; they are now my targeted users. I spoke with a total of 1 male and 5 females. Key questions I asked included:

  • Tell me about the last time you used Delta.com.
  • What are you trying to accomplish when using Delta website?
  • Are you able to do everything you expect you should be able to do on the site?
  • Are there any improvements that you would like to see?

One of, ultimately, the most valuable follow up questions that I posed revolved around the use of Delta Vacations, a service that bundles flight/car/hotel. Responses to this followup, through data synthesization and analysis, proved to be a dilemma.

Delta customers value the SkyMiles program and are brand loyal. Users often use the website to plan out future trips even without set dates or funds.

After interviewing, I used affinity mapping to synthesize the data. After grouping similar points of data, I was able to identify several common key attributes/characteristics and behaviors. I ultimately discovered enough information to create a persona of my targeted user group.

It is important to NOTE that this persona is representative of the data collected during User Interviews. This persona represents ONLY those interviewed; 4 of the 6 interviewees were retired and thus the data is represented in the persona developed. It should be noted that given further resources and interviews that another persona would undoubtedly emerge.

User persona, Sandra, is a 34 yo retired globetrotting millennial. She is a Delta Loyalist who likes to travel at least 3-4 times a year. She views taking trips as a hobby and frequently visits Delta.com to plan trips. She is a SkyMiles member and values the program. She prefers to book on the web, but uses the application to access boarding documents, skymiles metrics, etc. She loves that Delta.com matches the sophistication and class that she experiences on board. She hates feeling confused and prefers to know all of her options.

Utilizing Sandra I was able to develop a problem space to work in and swiftly begin crafting a tailored solution through prototyping.

Even with a great website, there is always room for improvement - I needed to utilize different UX research techniques to quantify the problem.

I reviewed the exploratory findings to determine possible opportunities that I might pursue. With the website being so well-done, it was difficult to uncover the area where I could actually make an improvement. At this point, I only knew that users, while enticed by the SkyMiles program, were often using the website to plan vacations; however, they were neither aware of nor using the Delta Vacations section of the website.

Current State User Flow does not lead to a smooth experience. Users forced to dig for information and/or forced to visit an external site.

I created a scenario and task to see how users are currently navigating the website in an attempt to complete the task.

The task asked users to assist Sandra in booking a bundled vacation for herself and her family to Paris in March. She needed flights, a car, and a hotel. What was interesting about this is how long it took users to find this information. While sitting in the airport waiting for my flight, I conducted user testing with 10 real life Delta customers, timing them on how long it took them to get to the correct page. On average, in the current state, it took 53.2 seconds.

During this stage of testing, I was able to hear more frustrations with the site; irritations with information sorting, annoyance of lack of promotion/highlighting for Delta Vacations and others. In fact, of the 10 users tested in the airport, none were aware of the Delta Vacations Program.

The current flow provided that users navigate to a subcategory (Travel Info) of the Navigation Bar before discovering the Delta Vacations link, buried in the third column. Users were also able to navigate by scrolling and clicking a button labeled “Vacation Deals” which took them to an external site (meetdeltavacations.com) only to link them back to the Delta Vacations page (hosted on Delta.com) to complete the task. This proves problematic for many reasons, primarily because its not best practice to lead customers away from your site.

What is the ultimate problem?

At this point it was abundantly clear. Why is Delta Vacations not showcased on the website? How might I be able to ease user frustrations navigating to this service and increase Delta revenue at the same time?

User Flow remedied to create straightforward path to complete task.

Armed with this information, I was able to create a proposed New User Flow which would quickly lead consumers to the Delta Vacations page.

I examined my research and findings and came to the conclusion that Delta Vacations needed it’s own section in the Navigation bar. I immediately created mock-ups and completed another round of user testing which confirmed that my proposed New User Flow alleviated the frustrations of finding the Delta Vacations page.

User testing of Prototype showed New User Flow allows for completion of task 5X faster.

Once I knew that my proposed changes were effective, I completed a working prototype showcasing the changes. I took this prototype and completed 10 more in-person user tests with a different focus group.

By incorporating my suggested changes, I was able to reduce the amount of time spent getting to the Delta Vacations page from a 53.2 second average to a 10.4 second average. That is a decrease in time taken to complete the task of 80%.

Prototype testing confirmed that my modifications were effective, and also provided me with stylistic and aesthetic suggestions on how to improve the design. These suggestions were weighed and implemented in the final client deliverable.

With my solution, Delta.com users are more readily exposed to Delta Vacations and can utilize the service in fewer clicks.

Ultimately, I worked diligently to uncover usability problems and an under-tapped revenue source for a multi-billion dollar well oiled machine. I produced a modified user flow chart as well as several stages of prototypes (both mobile and desktop) to meet the needs of the client as well as satisfy users needs for usability.

My solution was multifaceted - first, I added a new tab/button for “Delta Vacations” to the navigation bar on the desktop website. With center placement and Delta Red text, this will immediately grab the attention of users that otherwise wouldn't know this service exists. Furthermore, this will reduce the amount of clicks required to access the service. Also, the current state provides that there is a link in the body of the main page that takes users to meetdeltavacations.com; a site that ultimately links users back to delta.com to complete the transaction. This unnecessary step has been removed as well. Similarly, in mobile view, Delta Vacations (again in Delta Red) is given its own tab in the hamburger menu.

Soft skills:
